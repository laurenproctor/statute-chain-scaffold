Cocaine

Coca leaf alkaloid and its primary metabolite ecgonine. Analysis compares New York Public Health Law scheduling against the federal Controlled Substances Act, surfacing how the two statutes treat chemical scope language for this substance.

Schedule class Schedule II
Chemical family Tropane alkaloid

01 Scope determination
New York scope is broader than federal
0.82 Confidence

New York Public Health Law § 3306(II)(b)(4)✓ reaches all salts, isomers, and salts of isomers of cocaine and ecgonine without qualifying the isomer term. Federal scheduling under the Controlled Substances Act limits isomer coverage to the levorotatory form per 21 U.S.C. § 802(14)(A)✓. The unqualified state term is the primary scope driver; ecgonine naming and derivative language provide secondary broadening signals.

Key divergence

New York's use of unqualified "isomers" is the primary scope driver. The term carries no optical/positional/geometric qualifier, which under a broad reading reaches all four isomer sub-types.

Federal § 802(14)(A) narrows the term to the optical isomer only for Schedule II, producing a textual gap for dextrorotatory cocaine variants and any non-optical stereoisomer forms.

The "shall not include" clause applies narrowly to decocainized leaves, not to any isomer term — preserving the full breadth of the scope language above it.

04 Statutory text
New York · primary provision
Schedule II — coca leaves, cocaine, and ecgonine
N.Y. Pub. Health Law § 3306 · Schedule II(b)(4)

Coca leaves and any salt, compound, derivative, or preparation of coca leaves, and any salt, compound, derivative, or preparation thereof which is chemically equivalent or identical with any of these substances including cocaine and ecgonine, their salts, isomers, and salts of isomers, except that the substances shall not include:

(A) decocainized coca leaves or extraction of coca leaves, which extractions do not contain cocaine or ecgonine; or

(B) {123I} ioflupane.

§ 812(b)(2) Schedule II (listing). Unless specifically excepted or unless listed in another schedule, any of the following substances whether produced directly or indirectly by extraction from substances of vegetable origin, or independently by means of chemical synthesis, or by a combination of extraction and chemical synthesis: coca leaves and any salt, compound, derivative, or preparation of coca leaves, and any salt, compound, derivative, or preparation thereof which is chemically equivalent or identical with any of these substances…

§ 802(14) "Isomer" defined. As used in this subchapter — (A) The term "isomer" means the optical isomer, except as used in schedule I(c) and paragraph (C) of this subsection.

(C) [For Schedule I hallucinogens only:] the term "isomer" means any optical, positional, or geometric isomer.

05 Precedent anchors

Matched precedents from the curated case database. Anchors contribute a small upward adjustment to the confidence score but are not determinative — the score remains driven by textual mismatch. Anchors sharpen confidence where courts have already ruled, but cannot override the underlying scope analysis.

Harbin v. Sessions✓ Categorical approach
5th Cir. 2017 · No. 16-60272

Court held that a state cocaine statute using unqualified isomer language was categorically broader than the federal CSA definition because it encompassed cocaine variants not covered federally. Directly on point for unqualified "isomers" language in Schedule II provisions.

Moncrieffe v. Holder✓ Framework
569 U.S. 184 · 2013

Affirmed that the categorical approach requires comparing the minimum conduct criminalized by the state statute against the federal generic offense. Establishes the analytical framework under which the isomer scope question is evaluated.

This analysis was generated under Greyledge methodology v.02, which scores textual mismatch between state and federal scheduling language along three dimensions: chemistry, federal text, and gap.
