Categorical approach  /  Client memorandum

NY Penal § 220.16
vs. 21 USCS § 802.

An overbreadth analysis of isomer classification under the categorical approach, confined to the text of the 2004 statutes.

Jessa Howes
Greyledge
April 2026
v.01

01   Framing

The categorical approach turns on a single question.

A state drug conviction serves as a federal predicate only if every offense the state statute criminalizes is also criminalized by the federal comparator. If NY Penal § 220.16 reaches any substance or isomer class not reached by 21 USCS § 802 and its schedules, § 220.16 is categorically overbroad. No § 220.16 conviction qualifies.

The analysis that follows is confined to what appears on the face of the two uploaded documents: 21 USCS § 802 (2004) and NY Penal § 220.16 (2004). Because § 220.16 is a penalty statute that depends on external definitions, the analysis identifies where the overbreadth argument is strongest on the face of these provisions and flags exactly what extrinsic sources must be consulted to close the proof.

Thesis

Two textual overbreadth arguments are visible on the face of these 2004 documents. The cocaine-isomer argument under § 220.16(1) and (12) rests on the closed list in § 802(17)(D): federal law reaches only optical and geometric isomers of cocaine, while NY’s cocaine schedule entry uses unqualified “isomers” language. The methamphetamine-isomer argument under § 220.16(7) works the same way through § 802(14)’s Schedule II(a)(4) rule. Both arguments depend on confirming the 2004 text of NY PHL §§ 3302 and 3306.

02   Side by side  ·  Isomer rules

The federal rule is tiered. The NY rule, on this face, is not.

Federal § 802(14) establishes three isomer regimes: a default (optical only); an expansion for Schedule I(c) hallucinogens (optical + positional + geometric — the broadest federal reach); and a limited expansion for Schedule II(a)(4) stimulants (optical + geometric only, positional excluded). For cocaine specifically, § 802(17)(D) hard-codes a closed list at optical + geometric. NY Penal § 220.16 contains no internal isomer definition. It borrows whatever PHL § 3302 provides.

03   Overbreadth by subdivision

Each § 220.16 subdivision is analyzed independently.

§ 220.16 plausibly treats each of its thirteen subdivisions as an alternative element of substance type and/or weight threshold. Under Taylor v. United States, 495 U.S. 575 (1990), the sentencing court may, in a narrow range of cases, consult the charging paper and jury instructions to identify which alternative element formed the basis of the conviction. The analysis below is therefore subdivision-by-subdivision, not against § 220.16 as a whole — designed to support a Taylor-style elements inquiry into the specific subdivision of conviction.

04   Argument skeleton

A novel argument, textually supported, on 2004 authority.

A note on scope

This analysis is confined to authority available as of the end of 2004. The categorical-approach doctrine has since developed substantially — much of the modern overbreadth caselaw, including decisions specifically addressing cocaine-isomer mismatches, postdates 2004 and is not cited here. The argument that follows was available in 2004 on the text of the statutes, but it was not yet supported by the robust body of post-2004 appellate precedent that would later validate it.

A. Legal standard

A state offense serves as a federal predicate only if its elements are the same as, or narrower than, those of the generic federal offense. The sentencing court looks to the statutory elements, not to the defendant’s actual conduct. If the state statute reaches conduct the federal definition does not, the state conviction is categorically overbroad and cannot serve as a predicate.

Authority: Taylor v. United States, 495 U.S. 575, 600–02 (1990) (adopting the categorical approach; sentencing court looks to “the fact of conviction and the statutory definition of the prior offense, rather than to the particular underlying facts”); Leocal v. Ashcroft, 543 U.S. 1, 7 (2004) (applying categorical approach; court examines “the elements and the nature of the offense of conviction, rather than to the particular facts relating to the crime”).

Under Taylor, the categorical approach still permits a sentencing court to consult the charging paper and jury instructions in the narrow range of cases where those documents actually required the jury to find all the elements of the generic federal offense — what later developed into the modified categorical approach for divisible statutes. As of 2004, the precise contours of that inquiry were still being worked out by the lower courts.

B. Argument 1 · Cocaine isomers under “narcotic drug” — § 220.16(1), (12)

§ 220.16(1) and (12) reach possession of a “narcotic drug” (with intent to sell), and possession of mixtures containing a narcotic drug at ½ ounce or more. “Narcotic drug” under New York law draws its scope from NY Public Health Law §§ 3302 and 3306, which include cocaine.

The federal comparator for cocaine is § 802(17)(D): “Cocaine, its salts, optical and geometric isomers, and salts of isomers.” This is a closed list. Positional isomers of cocaine are not reached by § 802(17)(D); only optical and geometric isomers are.

The textual mismatch

On the face of the 2004 statutes, the NY Schedule II entry for cocaine (PHL § 3306, Schedule II(b)(4)) reaches “cocaine and ecgonine, their salts, isomers, and salts of isomers” — without limiting “isomers” to optical or geometric. Other entries in the same schedule (e.g., hallucinogenic substances at § 3306(d)) expressly do limit “isomer” to “optical, position and geometric.” The contrast is instructive: when the NY legislature meant to cap isomer scope, it said so. For cocaine, it did not.

Applied through Taylor’s elements-based analysis, the NY cocaine definition reaches a class of isomers — positional — that § 802(17)(D) does not. That is a textual categorical mismatch.

To close: Confirm the 2004 text of PHL §§ 3302 and 3306; identify a NY prosecution, regulation, or schedule entry indicating that NY in fact controls positional isomers of cocaine that federal law omits.

C. Argument 2 · Methamphetamine isomers — § 220.16(7)

§ 220.16(7) criminalizes possession with intent to sell of “methamphetamine, its salts, isomers or salts of isomers” in aggregate weights of ⅛ ounce or more. The term “isomers” is unqualified on the face of the statute.

The federal comparator is Schedule II(a)(4). Its isomer scope is set by § 802(14): “As used in schedule II(a)(4), the term ‘isomer’ means any optical or geometric isomer.” Positional isomers are affirmatively excluded.

The textual mismatch

If NY Public Health Law § 3302 or the relevant § 3306 schedule entry, in their 2004 form, define or apply “isomer” without limitation, § 220.16(7) criminalizes possession of positional isomers of methamphetamine that Schedule II(a)(4) does not reach. This is the second textual mismatch visible on the face of the uploaded documents.

To close: Confirm the 2004 text of PHL §§ 3302 and 3306 methamphetamine entry; confirm whether NY’s schedule uses unqualified “isomers” language for methamphetamine or specifies a type.

D. Argument 3 · Undefined categories — “stimulant,” “hallucinogenic substance”

§ 220.16 uses the bare terms “stimulant,” “hallucinogen,” and “hallucinogenic substance” without definition. 21 USCS § 802 does not define “stimulant” or “hallucinogenic substance” either. Federal coverage of these categories depends entirely on the schedules.

The overbreadth question is whether NY’s 2004 statutory lists of stimulants and hallucinogenic substances — in PHL § 3306 or implementing regulations — include any substance not listed in the 2004 federal Schedules I–V. The fact that NY distinguishes “hallucinogen” from “hallucinogenic substance” with separate weight thresholds suggests two separate lists and increases the likelihood of at least one substance-level mismatch.

E. Weaker angle · LSD and Schedule I(c) hallucinogens

LSD is expressly named in § 802(9)(C) and sits in Schedule I(c), where § 802(14) reaches optical, positional, and geometric isomers. Federal breadth is already maximal for Schedule I(c) substances. A pure isomer-overbreadth theory will not succeed here. Any argument would have to identify a specific hallucinogen on NY’s 2004 list that is absent from the 2004 federal Schedule I — a scheduling theory, not an isomer theory.

F. Practical posture in 2004

The isomer-overbreadth argument was novel in 2004. Taylor’s categorical framework was the doctrinal foundation, and Leocal reinforced its elements-based focus earlier that year. But no Supreme Court decision had yet squarely addressed whether a one-step-broader state isomer definition was sufficient to defeat a federal predicate, and no circuit had yet issued a published decision accepting the cocaine-isomer textual-mismatch argument. Counsel advancing this argument in 2004 would be making a textual case without robust appellate authority behind it.

The argument’s strength in 2004 rests on four points: (i) the statutes’ plain text; (ii) Taylor’s requirement that the sentencing court look to the elements, not the conduct; (iii) Congress’s deliberate choice to limit § 802(17)(D) to a closed list of optical and geometric cocaine isomers; and (iv) NY’s parallel deliberate choice, visible in the structure of PHL § 3306, to cap isomer scope in some schedule entries but not for cocaine.

G. Vehicle and divisibility

§ 220.16 is plausibly divisible across its thirteen subdivisions, each setting out an alternative element of substance type and/or weight threshold. Taylor permitted a sentencing court, in a narrow range of cases, to consult the charging paper and jury instructions to determine which alternative element supported the conviction. As of 2004, the lower courts were working out how broadly that permission ran; the framework later formalized around the concept of a “divisible” statute had not yet been articulated by the Supreme Court. The subdivision-level framing in Section 03 is built to support a Taylor-style inquiry into which subdivision of § 220.16 was the basis of the conviction.
