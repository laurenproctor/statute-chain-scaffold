01Scope

A scored companion, bounded by 2004.

This analysis applies the Greyledge scoring methodology (v.02) to the same two provisions examined in the companion Isomer Overreach Analysis: NY Penal § 220.16 (specifically subdivisions (1), (7), and (12)) and 21 USCS § 802 with its incorporated schedules. The goal is to translate the narrative argument-skeleton of the companion document into a quantified confidence score per the methodology's framework.

Two constraints carry through from the companion:

Constraint one — 2004 text only. All statutory language analyzed is the text in force as of the end of 2004. The NY PHL § 3302 definition of "narcotic drug," the NY PHL § 3306 Schedule II(b)(4) cocaine entry, and the federal § 802(14) and § 802(17)(D) isomer provisions are all analyzed as they stood in 2004. Post-2004 amendments (e.g., the 2012 addition of the ioflupane exception, anabolic-steroid redefinitions) are excluded.

Constraint two — factual, not predictive. Per the methodology's own statement of limits (section 09), a confidence score measures textual mismatch, not legal outcome. The scores below describe the state of the 2004 text. They do not forecast how any court would rule, then or now.

A note on precedent anchors

The methodology permits a small upward adjustment to a confidence score where curated precedent supports the provision under analysis. Under the 2004-only scope constraint, no such anchor applies to either pathway: the pre-2005 appellate record does not contain a published decision squarely holding a state cocaine or methamphetamine definition categorically overbroad on isomer grounds. Taylor v. United States, 495 U.S. 575 (1990), supplies the doctrinal frame but no substance-specific holding. All scores below therefore report the unanchored textual measurement.

02Methodology recap

Terms, weights, and how they combine.

The full methodology is documented separately in Greyledge · Methodology v.02. What follows is the condensed reference needed to read the scores in sections 04 and 05.

Base weights

Each scope-relevant term in a state statute carries a base weight reflecting how strongly its presence signals textual mismatch with federal scheduling. Weights are derived from three components — chemistry precision, federal text comparator, and resulting gap — and range from 0.90 (positional isomer, the cleanest mismatch) to 0.10 (boilerplate coverage terms with complete federal parity).

Term Base Why
Positional isomer 0.90 Cleanest mismatch. Federal § 802(14) excludes positional for non-hallucinogens.
Stereoisomer 0.82 Umbrella category. Partial federal coverage via optical default.
Optical isomer (all forms) 0.75 Federal default reaches only the pharmacologically active form.
Geometric isomer 0.70 Class-dependent. Real for most schedules; closes for hallucinogens.
Isomers (unqualified) 0.60 Interpretive contingency. Scope depends on how a court reads "isomer" generically.
Derivative 0.45 Loose chemistry. Federal use varies substance-by-substance.
Salts of isomers 0.25 Near-universal parity across state and federal.
Compound / preparation 0.10 Boilerplate. Same framing on both sides.

Context modifiers (Schedule II)

Because federal § 802(14) defines "isomer" differently across schedules, the same state term creates a different gap for a Schedule II drug than for a Schedule I hallucinogen. Modifiers adjust the base weight at scoring time. Both cocaine and methamphetamine are Schedule II drugs, so Schedule II modifiers apply throughout.

The gap with NY's unqualified "isomers" language is therefore only the positional fraction.

What the score means for cocaine

A 0.77 reads as follows within the methodology's scale: the state provision contains substantial scope-broadening material relative to the federal comparator, but the broadening runs through an unqualified term ("isomers") rather than through an explicit positional-isomer designation. A hypothetical state statute that named "positional isomers" directly would score in the 0.90+ range. NY's 2004 language does not reach that ceiling; it lands solidly in the meaningful-mismatch band, with the gap concentrated in the interpretive space of how an unqualified "isomers" is read.

Two features of the 2004 NY text sharpen the argument that the unqualified reading is the correct one. First, NY's parallel schedule entries for hallucinogens and stimulants (PHL § 3306(d), § 3306(c)) expressly define "isomer" to include "optical, position and geometric isomers." The drafters knew how to specify. Second, for cocaine, they did not specify — leaving "isomers, and salts of isomers" unqualified on the face of the Schedule II entry. Under standard canons, the deliberate choice to specify in some entries and not others is evidence that the unqualified term carries its generic chemistry meaning.

05Methamphetamine · scored

§ 220.16(7) · the standalone methamphetamine subdivision.

NY Penal § 220.16(7) criminalizes possession with intent to sell of "methamphetamine, its salts, isomers or salts of isomers" in aggregate weights of ⅛ ounce or more. Unlike the cocaine pathway, § 220.16(7) contains its own isomer language on its face — it does not route through PHL § 3306. The federal comparator is Schedule II(a)(4) as modified by § 802(14)'s specific expansion for that entry.

Class-specific adjustment explained

The −0.05 column reflects federal Schedule II(a)(4)'s expansion under § 802(14): "As used in schedule II(a)(4), the term 'isomer' means any optical or geometric isomer." Federal law therefore reaches both optical and geometric isomers of methamphetamine (not just optical, as for most Schedule II substances). The practical gap between unqualified state "isomers" and this expanded federal scope is narrower — only the positional fraction. The methodology accommodates this via a small downward adjustment to the effective weight of the unqualified-isomers term for this specific class.

What the score means for methamphetamine

The methamphetamine pathway scores within a percentage point of the cocaine pathway, but the path there is different. On cocaine, the federal side is the closed list (§ 802(17)(D): optical + geometric, period), and the state broadens via an unqualified term that includes derivatives and compounds. On methamphetamine, the federal side is already one step broader than the Sched II default (§ 802(14) adds geometric for this class), which closes part of the gap — but the state side also has only a single unqualified "isomers" term to work with, not a full schedule entry with additional coverage language. The lower term count on the state side approximately offsets the narrower federal scope on the federal side.

The practical implication: the two arguments have different shapes. The cocaine argument leans on the fact that § 802(17)(D) is a closed list, and on NY's parallel decision to specify isomer types for some schedule entries but not cocaine. The methamphetamine argument leans on the fact that § 220.16(7) itself — the charging subdivision — uses unqualified "isomers" on its face, without needing to route through PHL § 3306 at all.

Why some substances score N/A

The methodology measures textual mismatch in isomer-scope language. For substances where the federal schedule already reaches all isomer types (Schedule I(c) hallucinogens, including LSD), the gap is closed by the federal text; an isomer-scope argument has nothing to measure. For substances where the charging subdivision uses no isomer language at all (phencyclidine in § 220.16(13)), the methodology likewise has no textual signal to score. The N/A tag is factual, not a failure — it flags that the isomer theory is not the right vector for that substance, and that any overbreadth argument there must proceed on a scheduling theory instead (is the substance on NY's list at all; does NY's list include substances federal law omits).

07Opiates · scored

§ 220.16(1), (12) · the opiate component of "narcotic drug."

"Narcotic drug" under § 220.16 also reaches opium and opiates — the other major component, alongside cocaine, of federal § 802(17). The federal comparator is § 802(17)(A): "Opium, opiates, derivatives of opium and opiates, including their isomers, esters, ethers, salts, and salts of isomers, esters, and ethers, whenever the existence of such isomers, esters, ethers, and salts is possible within the specific chemical designation." Why the score is lower than cocaine's

The opiates pathway contains the same unqualified "isomer" language as cocaine — but the derivative term, which contributed modestly to cocaine's score (effective weight 0.25), contributes almost nothing here. The reason is parity: federal § 802(17)(A) itself expressly reaches "derivatives of opium and opiates," so the state's use of "derivative" creates no gap with the federal text. An additional −0.15 parity adjustment on top of the standard Sched II −0.20 modifier reduces the derivative term to near-zero effective weight.

The opiates side also lacks a federal closed-list provision analogous to § 802(17)(D). Where § 802(17)(D) hard-caps cocaine at "optical and geometric isomers, and salts of isomers" (a textually closed list), § 802(17)(A) uses the looser formulation "including their isomers … whenever the existence of such isomers … is possible within the specific chemical designation," which then falls back to the § 802(14) default rule (optical only). The state's unqualified "isomer" still creates a gap against federal-optical-only, but without the closed-list sharpness that makes the cocaine argument textually vivid.

Practical implication for counsel

A § 220.16(1) or (12) conviction premised on an opiate rather than cocaine is a meaningfully weaker vehicle for the isomer-overbreadth argument than a cocaine-premised conviction. Where the Shepard-document record permits identifying the specific substance of conviction, counsel defending a narcotic-drug conviction on this theory should prefer to work with a cocaine-based record. Where the record is opiate-based, the argument still exists but runs through the unqualified "isomer" term in PHL § 3302(20)(b) alone, without the closed-list reinforcement that makes the cocaine pathway distinctive.

08Flags triggered

Four conditions the score alone does not capture.

Per methodology section 07, flags operate alongside the confidence score as categorical indicators. They surface scope conditions that warrant attorney review but cannot be cleanly reduced to a number. A provision can score high and also trigger flags, or trigger flags without adding meaningfully to the score. The flags below apply across the pathways analyzed in sections 04, 05, and 07.

Flag — Interpretive Contingency
All three scored pathways use unqualified "isomers" language
The cocaine pathway (via PHL § 3306(II)(b)(4)), the methamphetamine pathway (on the face of § 220.16(7) itself), and the opiates pathway (via PHL § 3302(20)(b)) all use "isomer" or "isomers" without qualifier. The broadening power of each depends on whether a court reads the term according to its generic chemistry meaning (all four sub-types) or narrows it to track federal § 802(14). The base weight of 0.60 accounts for this contingency, but the flag makes it explicit so the interpretive question is not buried inside a number. Counsel should be prepared to argue the canon that a deliberate drafting choice to specify isomer type in some provisions and not others supports the generic reading.
Flag — Inherited Category Language
Cocaine and opiate scope derive from parent statutory definitions, not from § 220.16
§ 220.16(1) and (12) criminalize possession of a "narcotic drug" without defining that term internally. The scope of "narcotic drug" as applied to cocaine is inherited from NY PHL § 3302(20) and § 3306(II)(b)(4). Its scope as applied to opiates is inherited from PHL § 3302(20)(a)–(b). The analytical work happens at the Public Health Law level, not at the § 220.16 level. Counsel should know which text is doing the work — charging and jury-instruction documents for a § 220.16(1) or (12) conviction will cite the Penal Law subdivision, but the overbreadth argument runs through the Public Health Law definitions and schedules.
Flag — Scheduling-Theory Substance (opposite of isomer theory)
Some § 220.16 substances are N/A for isomer analysis but may support a scheduling theory
LSD, "hallucinogen," "hallucinogenic substance," and phencyclidine all score N/A under the isomer methodology — either because federal Schedule I(c) already reaches all isomer types (closing the gap for LSD and hallucinogens) or because § 220.16 uses no isomer language on its face (PCP in subdivision 13). The scheduling theory is a separate inquiry: whether NY's actual list of substances under PHL § 3306 includes a particular substance that the federal schedule omits. This is a substance-by-substance comparison of the full 2004 schedules — a different analysis than the textual-mismatch measurement produced by this methodology, and one the isomer scoring framework deliberately does not attempt.
Flag — No Precedent Anchor (2004-bounded)
No pre-2005 appellate decision holds any of the scored pathways categorically overbroad
Under the 2004-only scope constraint, the methodology's precedent-anchor input is zero for all three scored pathways. Taylor v. United States, 495 U.S. 575 (1990), supplies the categorical-approach framework, and Leocal v. Ashcroft, 543 U.S. 1 (2004), confirms the elements-based inquiry — but neither is a substance-specific anchor. This flag is separate from the confidence score because the methodology deliberately decouples textual measurement from litigation record. A high score without a precedent anchor means the textual case is strong but has not yet been tested in published appellate precedent at the relevant time horizon.

Reading the distribution

Of the nine substances and category terms named directly in § 220.16, two land in the meaningful band, one in the contested band, and six are N/A under the isomer methodology (two because Schedule I(c)'s maximal federal isomer rule closes the gap; one because the subdivision itself uses no isomer language; three because they are category terms that resolve substance-by-substance rather than carry independent isomer-scope signal).

The isomer-theory overbreadth argument is a narrow instrument. It works for Schedule II substances where federal text names a closed isomer list or sticks to the § 802(14) default, and where the state text is unqualified. Most of § 220.16 is outside that window.

How to act on this distribution

Counsel working with a § 220.16 conviction should first identify which subdivision and which substance are on the Shepard-document record. If the record places the conviction in the meaningful band (methamphetamine under (7), or cocaine under (1)/(12)), the isomer-overbreadth argument is textually available and the confidence score indicates substantial supporting material. If the record places the conviction in the contested band (opiates under (1)/(12)), the argument still exists but is weaker, and counsel should evaluate whether the record permits a narrowing to cocaine instead. If the record places the conviction in the N/A zone, isomer theory is not the right vector — a scheduling theory (comparing NY's actual substance list against federal Schedules I–V) is the alternative framework, which is outside this methodology's scope.

10Limits

What these numbers are not.

The methodology's section 09 lays out four limits that apply to every confidence score it produces. They apply with equal force here, and several are sharpened by the 2004-only scope constraint.

Not a legal determination

A score of 0.77 does not say NY's cocaine definition is categorically overbroad. It says the textual evidence supports that argument at a meaningful-mismatch level on the 2004 text. Whether a court so holds depends on the jurisdiction, the case posture, the record, and the specific argumentative moves counsel makes. None of that is captured in the number.

Not a probability

0.77 does not mean a 77% chance of a favorable ruling. The figure is a textual-mismatch measurement on a 0-to-1 scale calibrated by the relative weights of detected terms. Two provisions at 0.77 could have very different litigation prospects; one at 0.77 could well lose while one at 0.65 wins, depending on factors the methodology deliberately excludes.

Does not predict outcomes

Litigation frequency, judicial receptiveness, and case outcomes are not inputs to the score. That design choice is deliberate — a triage tool that describes the textual landscape accurately is more useful than one that tries to predict outcomes and gets them wrong. Under the 2004-only constraint, this limit is especially salient: the post-2004 appellate record that would inform any outcome prediction for similar arguments is outside the scope of this analysis by construction.

Does not replace attorney review

The score surfaces provisions worth examining closely. Both NY pathways clearly qualify. What the score cannot do is tell counsel what the argument will look like in the specific case, what the best vehicle is, how to handle divisibility under Taylor, or how to brief the canon that deliberate specification in some provisions supports the generic reading in others. Those are attorney decisions that follow the triage, not replace it.

Use together

This scored analysis is a companion to the narrative Isomer Overreach Analysis, not a replacement for it. The narrative document develops the argument skeleton in doctrinal form — legal standard, argument-by-argument walkthrough, vehicle and divisibility. This document translates the textual evidence into a single quantified measurement. Together they provide both the argumentative scaffold and the numeric triage signal. Neither alone is sufficient; read as a pair they give counsel the textual case and the argument case in parallel.
