Methamphetamine

Synthetic amphetamine-class stimulant. Analysis compares New York Public Health Law scheduling against the federal Controlled Substances Act, which schedules methamphetamine across two different schedules based on formulation. The scope analysis for methamphetamine looks structurally different from other controlled substances because state and federal isomer language track closely here.

New York Public Health Law § 3306(II)(d)(2)✓ and federal 21 U.S.C. § 812✓ both use "salts, isomers, and salts of isomers" unqualified when scheduling methamphetamine. Under our three-component framework, this produces a low confidence score for scope broadening — the primary textual driver that supports isomer-based scope arguments is absent when state and federal language align this closely. The real divergence is elsewhere: federal law splits methamphetamine across Schedule II (injectable liquid) and Schedule III (all other forms), while New York places all methamphetamine in Schedule II. This schedule-placement mismatch is a separate analytical question surfaced under scope flags below.

Schedule placement mismatch
New York schedules all methamphetamine in Schedule II. Federal law splits it: injectable liquid forms are Schedule II under § 812(c) Schedule II, while all other forms are Schedule III under § 812(c) Schedule III(a)(3). A New York Schedule II methamphetamine conviction may not categorically match a federal Schedule II drug offense for sentencing or immigration purposes, because most forms of methamphetamine are federally Schedule III.
Realistic probability burden
Under Matter of Felix-Figueroa, 29 I&N Dec. 157 (BIA 2025)✓, even where textual overbreadth exists between state and federal methamphetamine definitions, the noncitizen must establish a "realistic probability" that the state would actually prosecute conduct involving isomers outside federal scope. Pointing to textual mismatch alone is not sufficient in most circuits. This burden applies to any scope argument a practitioner might build from this analysis.
L-methamphetamine reality
Methamphetamine has two optical isomers. The dextrorotatory form (d-methamphetamine) is the pharmacologically active controlled compound. The levorotatory form (l-methamphetamine) is present in over-the-counter nasal decongestants such as Vicks VapoInhaler. Both state and federal "isomers" language reach both forms textually, but the real-world prosecution question is whether l-meth is a meaningful basis for overbreadth arguments — United States v. Vega-Ortiz, 822 F.3d 1031 (9th Cir. 2016)✓ addressed this in the California context.

Key divergence

The textual scope language is nearly identical on both sides. Both New York and federal law use "including its salts, isomers, and salts of isomers" — the same unqualified isomer phrasing.

The meaningful divergence is schedule placement, not scope breadth. New York places all methamphetamine in Schedule II. Federal splits it: injectable liquid in Schedule II, everything else in Schedule III. This affects categorical analysis for sentencing enhancements that depend on federal schedule level.

A secondary divergence exists if a court reads New York's unqualified "isomers" to cover geometric isomers — Lorenzo v. Sessions supported this reading for California, but has not been tested in the Second Circuit for New York.

(d) Stimulants. Unless specifically excepted or unless listed in another schedule, any material, compound, mixture, or preparation which contains any quantity of the following substances having a stimulant effect on the central nervous system, including its salts, isomers, and salts of isomers:

(2) Methamphetamine.
(3) Phenmetrazine.
(4) Methylphenidate.
(5) Lisdexamfetamine.

05 Precedent anchors

Matched precedents from the curated case database. Anchors contribute a small upward adjustment to the confidence score but are not determinative — the score remains driven by textual mismatch. Anchors sharpen confidence where courts have already ruled, but cannot override the underlying scope analysis.

Lorenzo v. Sessions✓ Isomer scope
902 F.3d 930 · 9th Cir. 2018

Ninth Circuit held California's methamphetamine definition (Health & Safety Code § 11378) was categorically broader than the federal definition because unqualified state "isomer" language reached geometric isomers federal scope did not capture. Directly supports the geometric-isomer argument flagged in the evidence matrix above. Caveat: Lorenzo is 9th Circuit; New York sits in the 2nd Circuit, which has not addressed this question for methamphetamine.

Matter of Felix-Figueroa✓ Realistic probability
29 I&N Dec. 157 · BIA 2025

BIA ruled that even where textual overbreadth exists between state and federal methamphetamine scope, the noncitizen must show a "realistic probability" the state would actually prosecute the non-federal conduct. Practically, this means pointing to textual isomer mismatch alone is not enough — practitioners need actual prosecution examples. Significantly raises the bar for scope-based immigration defense involving methamphetamine in most circuits.

United States v. Vega-Ortiz✓ L-meth reality
822 F.3d 1031 · 9th Cir. 2016

Ninth Circuit held that a defendant arguing California's methamphetamine definition was overbroad because federal law excluded a particular OTC product containing l-methamphetamine (such as Vicks inhalers) must show a "realistic probability" of prosecution for that non-federally-controlled product. Informs the l-methamphetamine flag above — the textual coverage is acknowledged but prosecution is unlikely in either system.

Moncrieffe v. Holder✓ Framework
569 U.S. 184 · 2013

Supreme Court established the categorical approach as the governing framework for comparing state controlled-substance convictions to federal generic offenses. The "realistic probability" standard discussed in Moncrieffe at 205–06 is the doctrinal source of the burden applied in Felix-Figueroa above.

Methodology

This analysis was generated under Greyledge methodology v.02, which scores textual mismatch between state and federal scheduling language along three dimensions: chemistry, federal text, and gap. The confidence score is a triage signal, not a legal determination. It measures how strongly the text supports a broadening argument, not the likelihood any particular court will agree.
