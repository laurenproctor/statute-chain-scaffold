VERIFICATION · v.01
Transparency document · Isomer Analysis

What verification means

An honest accounting of what our verification process covers, what it does not, and how the "Verified" badge is earned. This page documents verification as it applies to the Isomer Analysis toolset. The underlying framework — what counts as verifiable, how sources are checked, how human review fills the gaps — is shared across all Greyledge toolsets.

00 Scope of this document

What this page covers

Greyledge is a platform for legal research across multiple analytical domains. This page documents verification for the Isomer Analysis toolset — the system that compares state and federal controlled-substance scheduling language to surface scope mismatches. Each Greyledge toolset has its own verification page, because the authoritative sources and verifiable claims differ by domain.

What remains consistent across toolsets is the underlying framework: what counts as a verifiable fact, how sources are selected, how verification timestamps are communicated, how failures are handled, and what kinds of claims require human review instead of API checks. If you've read the verification page for one Greyledge toolset, the framework described here will be familiar — only the specific sources and claim types will differ.

Scope-specific sources, shared framework. Each toolset's verification page is tailored to the facts that toolset asserts; the standard for what verification means is the same across Greyledge.

For the Isomer Analysis toolset specifically, the verification process checks citations, statute text, and chemical identifiers against four authoritative public sources. The sections below explain exactly what is and is not covered, which sources are used, how often verification runs, and what happens when it fails.

01 What verification covers

What we verify

Every Greyledge substance page contains two kinds of claims. The first kind — factual, discrete, externally checkable — is what verification covers. The second kind — analytical, interpretive, methodology-driven — is not verifiable by any third-party service and is addressed separately (see what we don't verify).

The verification process checks every claim in the first category against authoritative public sources. If a claim cannot be matched to an authoritative source, the page does not display a verified badge for that claim.

What is verified
  • Case citations Does Harbin v. Sessions exist at 5th Cir. 2017? Is the docket number correct?
  • Federal statute citations Does 21 U.S.C. § 812(b)(2) exist and resolve to the cited provision?
  • State statute citations Does N.Y. Pub. Health § 3306(II)(b)(4) exist and reach cocaine scheduling?
  • Block-quoted statutory text Does the displayed text match the authoritative current-law version?
  • Citation format validity Is the reporter, volume, and page number real and internally consistent?
  • Chemical identifier references Is cocaine's CAS number correct? Does it link to the right PubChem record?
What is not verified
  • The scope determination Whether the state statute is actually broader than federal is an analytical conclusion, not a verifiable fact.
  • Weight assignments Our methodology assigns each term its weight based on chemistry, federal text, and gap. No API can validate our reasoning.
  • Scope flag determinations Whether "interpretive contingency" or "analog language" applies is a judgment call the system makes, not a fact to check.
  • The confidence score The 0.82 is a measurement of textual mismatch under our framework — it is not a fact that exists independently.
  • Legal reasoning Whether the analysis supports any specific legal argument, or how any court might rule, cannot be verified.
  • Interpretive characterizations of case law A case's existence is verifiable. Our summary of what it held is analytical work by Greyledge and a reviewing attorney.
Verification means our citations and quoted text match authoritative public sources. It does not mean our analysis is correct, that any court will agree with it, or that it substitutes for legal judgment.
02 The limits of automated verification

What verification cannot tell you

Even in the category of "verifiable facts," automated verification has real limits worth naming explicitly. Users who rely on the verified badge should understand them.

Verification is a snapshot, not a continuous check

The verification that produced the badge on a substance page happened at a specific point in time. The badge timestamp reflects that. Statute texts and case records can change after verification runs. If a provision is amended or a case is overturned, the badge does not update automatically until verification runs again (see how often).

API sources can themselves be incomplete or behind

The government APIs we use are authoritative but not always perfectly current. Recently enacted amendments may take days or weeks to appear in the official sources. When we verify against a source that has not yet ingested a recent change, we may match against the superseded version and not know it.

Matching is textual, not semantic

A statute block-quote is verified by checking that the text matches the authoritative version character-for-character. This catches typos, OCR errors, and paraphrasing but does not catch context errors. If we correctly quote subsection (b)(4) but label it as subsection (b)(5), the text will match but the reference will be wrong. Human review catches these errors; verification alone does not.

Case outcome interpretations are summaries, not quotes

When we describe what Harbin v. Sessions held, that description is our summary. The verification badge confirms the case exists at the cited reporter — not that our description of its holding is accurate. Interpretation of case law is reviewed by attorneys on the Greyledge team, not by any API.

03 Authoritative sources

The sources we verify against

Four public sources, each maintained by a government body or established nonprofit. We document each source used, when it was checked, and what response it returned in the verification log (accessible from the badge on each substance page).

CourtListener
Free Law Project · 501(c)(3)
Used for case citations. Verifies that a cited case exists, normalizes the citation format (catches variations like "576 US 644" versus "576 U.S. 644"), and returns the canonical case record. Built on the Eyecite library developed with the Harvard Library Innovation Lab, which has been trained on over 50 million legal citations.
Cases only · Not statutes
GovInfo API
U.S. Government Publishing Office
Used for federal statutes. Retrieves the authoritative current-law version of U.S. Code provisions. We compare block-quoted statute text character-for-character against the response. Also used for federal regulatory text and public laws.
Federal only
NY Open Legislation
NY Senate Technology Services
Used for New York statutes. Retrieves current and historical versions of NY state law provisions. Notably supports date-specific queries, allowing us to verify what a provision said on a specific date rather than only its current form — relevant for analyses tied to convictions under prior versions of the statute.
NY only
PubChem
National Institutes of Health
Used for chemical identifiers. Verifies CAS numbers, molecular formulas, and canonical chemical names. When a substance page asserts that cocaine is a tropane alkaloid, the linked PubChem record is the authoritative source for that classification.
Chemistry only
04 Verification schedule

How often verification runs

Verification runs at build time, not at page load time. When a substance page is first published, verification executes against all four sources. When any part of the page is edited, verification runs again before publication. Beyond that, a scheduled job re-verifies every substance page on a rolling basis.

Publication trigger

Any edit to a substance page triggers re-verification before the page can be published. This catches claims introduced during editing that would not have been present in the original publication verification.

Scheduled re-verification

Every substance page is re-verified at least monthly, with higher-traffic pages re-verified more frequently. The schedule is designed to catch statute amendments and case reversals within a reasonable window without over-consuming API rate limits.

Event-triggered re-verification

We monitor for statute amendments and case reversals through docketed legislative and judicial data feeds. When an event affects a provision or case cited on a Greyledge substance page, that page is queued for immediate re-verification regardless of schedule.

The verification badge on each substance page shows the timestamp of the most recent successful verification. Users can click through to see the full log, including which sources were checked and what each returned.

05 Badge states

What the badge states mean

A substance page's verification badge can appear in three states. Each reflects a specific condition and should be interpreted accordingly.

Verified

All checks passed. Every citation and block-quoted text on this page was successfully matched against its authoritative source within the last verification window. The timestamp indicates when this occurred.

Verification stale

Overdue for recheck. The last verification succeeded, but enough time has passed that it should be rechecked. Claims on the page have not changed, but authoritative sources may have updated. Treat this as a signal to consult the source directly for any time-sensitive decision.

Verification failed

At least one check did not match. The log identifies which claim or citation failed verification and what the discrepancy was. The page remains accessible but this state should be treated as a warning to verify the specific failing claim against the authoritative source before relying on it.

06 Failure handling

When verification fails

A verification failure is a signal, not a certainty. When verification fails on a claim, one of three things is true, and the failure triggers a specific response in each case.

The claim is wrong

If we have a typo in a citation, a misquoted statute passage, or a case cited to the wrong reporter, verification will fail and the page is flagged for correction. A reviewer either fixes the claim or removes it. The verification log preserves the record of the original failure and the correction.

The source has changed

A statute may have been amended since the page was last verified. A case may have been subsequently treated or reversed. In these cases, verification will show a discrepancy not because we are wrong now but because the authoritative source has moved. These failures trigger a review to update the page to reflect the current state of the law.

The API itself is unavailable or incorrect

External services occasionally have downtime or data-ingest lags. A failure that appears to be on the source side — not reproducible on a retry, or contradicted by other authoritative sources — is noted in the log but does not trigger a page warning state. Sustained source-side failures are escalated to the verification team.

In all three cases, the verification log records the failure, the diagnosis, and the resolution. Users can access the log from the badge on any substance page.

07 What only humans can check

Human review fills the gaps

Automated verification covers citations, statute text, and chemical identifiers. It cannot cover the analytical work that makes a substance page useful. That work is reviewed by attorneys and, where relevant, by chemists with subject-matter expertise.

Attorney review

Every substance page is reviewed by at least one licensed attorney before publication. The review covers the scope determination, the weight applications, the scope flag triggers, the case holding summaries, and the narrative text describing the analysis. The reviewing attorney is identified in the page's metadata along with the date of review.

Subject-matter review for chemistry

Chemistry claims that go beyond what can be verified against PubChem — claims about what counts as a derivative, which isomers are pharmacologically active, how scope terms behave across drug classes — are reviewed by a consulting chemist. Not every page requires this review; when it applies, the chemist is identified and the scope of the review is recorded.

Methodology review

The methodology document itself — the three-component framework, the weight table, the context modifier system — is not verified against any external source. It is work product by the Greyledge team and is subject to the governance and change control documented in the methodology. Methodology changes go through mandatory review and changelog entries before taking effect.

A verified page is one where the facts check out against authoritative sources and a qualified human has reviewed the analysis those facts support. Neither half is sufficient alone.
